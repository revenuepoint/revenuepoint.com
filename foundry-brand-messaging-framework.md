## 1. Executive Summary

### What Foundry Is

Foundry is a fully managed data orchestration and AI platform built by RevenuePoint for mid-market businesses. It connects every system a company runs on — CRM, ERP, accounting, inventory, and any other operational source — into a single managed data layer, then delivers live dashboards, AI-generated analysis, automated agents, and audited action execution across connected systems. Every pipeline, every dashboard, every agent, and every update is managed entirely by RevenuePoint. Clients use the Foundry portal. RevenuePoint runs everything that powers it.

Foundry is not a BI tool. It is not a data pipeline product. It is not an AI chatbot. It is the orchestration layer that ties all of those capabilities into one service — with one team accountable for the outcome.

### The Core Problem

Mid-market companies — those running on multiple systems with 50 to 500 employees — generate real operational data but have no reliable way to act on it. Their CRM, ERP, and accounting platform each report different numbers. The analyst who builds dashboards is overloaded, underresourced, and reconciling data manually every week. "Self-serve" BI tools turned out to require someone to build and maintain them — someone who doesn't exist on the team or already has a full-time job. And even when the dashboards are right, they describe what happened. Nothing responds to it.

The result: decisions get made on data that is late, wrong, or incomplete. Problems compound between the time they happen and the time someone notices them. And every attempt to fix it — another tool, another hire, another integration — adds another thing to manage without solving the underlying problem.

The underlying problem is the absence of orchestration: a single layer that connects the systems, manages the data, delivers the intelligence, takes the action, and is accountable for all of it.

### Why Now

The AI era created urgency. Every operations leader and finance leader now knows that AI-driven analysis and automated operations are possible. But the market delivered AI on top of fragmented infrastructure — producing inconsistent outputs, broken pipelines, and expensive disappointments. The "build your own modern data stack" path (Fivetran + Snowflake + dbt + Looker + a data engineer) proved too expensive, too complex, and too brittle for the mid-market. Companies are consolidating tools, not adding them. The category that's missing — and that no vendor in this segment has claimed — is fully managed orchestration: one partner who runs the full stack from ingestion to action, on a flat monthly fee, with named accountability.

## 3. Brand Personality & Voice

### Six Brand Personality Dimensions

**1. Operationally Precise**
We speak the language of operations. OEE. AR aging. Days sales outstanding. Scrap rate. Prescription throughput. Donor retention. We don't speak in abstractions like "business insights" when we mean "daily production yield broken down by line and shift." When we say "your data," we mean your production orders, your open invoices, your donor pipeline — specific objects in your specific business.

*In copy:* Use industry-specific objects and metrics wherever possible. "Your AR aging report" not "financial reporting." "Production orders and defect logs" not "manufacturing data."

---

**2. Service-Accountable**
We make specific commitments and we keep them. "Live in 6 weeks" is not an aspiration. "Named administrator" is not a call center. "We decline clients where we can't deliver ROI" is not a sales tactic. Every promise we make in marketing is a promise the delivery team can honor.

*In copy:* Favor specific, verifiable claims over superlatives. "Live in 6 weeks" beats "fast implementation." "One named administrator for your instance" beats "dedicated support."

---

**3. Confident Without Hype**
We don't call things revolutionary or game-changing. We describe what Foundry does and let the outcomes speak. We believe in what we've built, and that belief shows in specificity — not in adjective inflation. When we're confident, we state a fact. When we're proud, we share a proof point.

*In copy:* Replace every adjective-driven claim with a verifiable statement. "Foundry is the only fully managed platform in this category" is confident. "Foundry is a revolutionary AI platform" is hype. One invites scrutiny; the other deflects it.

---

**4. Accessible but Not Dumbed Down**
Our buyers are smart. They run complex businesses with real operational discipline. They understand what a CRM is, what AR aging means, what a PO-to-cash cycle looks like. They are not data engineers, and we don't pretend they need to be. We explain the technology only when the explanation increases confidence — not to showcase depth.

*In copy:* Skip technical internals (Airbyte, Airflow, dbt, Postgres) in hero and top-level sections. Bring them in for technical validation sections (how it works, implementation, FAQ) where depth builds confidence.

---

**5. Honest About Complexity**
We acknowledge that data is hard. That integration is messy. That most "easy setup" tools turned out not to be. We say: "This is genuinely difficult to do. We've made it not difficult for you." That honesty builds trust faster than false simplicity, especially with buyers who have scar tissue from previous deployments.

*In copy:* Name the actual pain before the solution. The problem sections of Foundry pages are among the most credible copy on the site. Don't soften the problem to get to the solution faster — the problem acknowledgment is the trust builder.

---

**6. Partner, Not Vendor**
RevenuePoint is in the business relationship for the outcome, not the transaction. We vet engagements. We decline clients where we don't see a clear path to value. We stay after delivery. This isn't a software license — it's a managed service with a named person, a monthly cadence, and accountability that extends beyond go-live. Everything Foundry-related should be written from a partner's perspective, not a vendor's.

*In copy:* Use "we manage" over "the platform handles." Reference named accountability: "A named RevenuePoint administrator manages your instance." The managed service relationship is a differentiator — name it explicitly.

### Voice & Tone Guide

| Dimension | Foundry Voice | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Register | Professional, grounded | Formal/stiff or startup-casual |

| Adjectives | Earn them with proof | Inflate with them |

## 4. Core Messaging Pillars

### Pillar 1: Orchestration, Not Assembly

**Statement:** Foundry manages the full loop — from data ingestion through intelligence delivery through automated action — as a single, accountable layer. Not a collection of tools stitched together. An orchestrated platform.

**The Underlying Insight**
Every mid-market company that tried to build a modern data stack discovered the same thing: the individual tools are fine. Fivetran pipes data. dbt transforms it. Looker visualizes it. But the integration surface between tools is where everything breaks. When Salesforce updates its schema, the Fivetran connector needs a fix. When dbt transforms a table, the Looker dashboard needs rebuilding. Each vendor supports their piece; nobody supports the seams. In mid-market companies, there's nobody whose job it is to own those seams. Foundry owns them — and that ownership is the product.

**Proof Points**
- Foundry runs on enterprise-grade components (Airbyte, Airflow, dbt, Postgres) — the same stack data teams at large companies build — but we manage it, not you.
- Blueprint ensures that upstream schema changes don't break downstream intelligence. When a source system adds a field, the semantic layer absorbs it. Otto and dashboards stay accurate.
- Flat monthly pricing includes pipeline maintenance. There is no bill for "connector repairs."

**Example Copy**

*Headline:* Every tool promises to connect your data. Foundry runs it.

*Subhead:* From pipelines to dashboards to agents — orchestrated end-to-end. Managed entirely by RevenuePoint.

*Body:* Building your own data stack means managing the seams between every tool. Foundry eliminates the seams. One managed layer from your CRM and ERP to your dashboards, agents, and actions. When something changes upstream — a new Salesforce field, a SAP update, a schema change — we handle it. Your team doesn't.

**Pillar Do's**
- Name "orchestration" explicitly — this is the category we're claiming
- Contrast with "stitching tools together" or "building your own stack"
- Explain what we run so the buyer understands the scope of management

**Pillar Don'ts**
- Don't lead with component names (Airbyte, Airflow) at the top of the funnel
- Don't say "unified" — say "orchestrated" or "connected"
- Don't make this a feature list — orchestration is a model, not a feature

---

### Pillar 2: Fully Managed Means Fully Accountable

**Statement:** RevenuePoint manages everything — pipelines, platform, dashboards, agents, updates, and ongoing optimization. You use the Foundry portal. We run everything that powers it.

**The Underlying Insight**
"Self-serve" was a lie told to buyers who couldn't afford an administrator. Every BI platform sold as self-serve — Power BI, Domo, ThoughtSpot — requires someone to build the dashboards, maintain the connectors, and fix it when something breaks. At mid-market companies, that person either doesn't exist or already has two other jobs. "Fully managed" is not a marketing differentiator — it is the direct answer to the most common reason data projects fail at this segment. When we say fully managed, we mean: we do it. Not setup and training. Not professional services. We run it month to month with a named person who knows your instance.

**Proof Points**
- Every Foundry client has a named RevenuePoint administrator and project manager. You know who to call.
- Flat monthly pricing covers pipelines, platform, dashboards, agents, and ongoing management. No usage overages, no hourly billing, no surprise scope changes.
- Live in 6 weeks. First dashboard in 2. Committed, not estimated.
- We decline clients where we don't see a clear path to ROI. That standard protects you — we only take engagements we can deliver.

**Example Copy**

*Headline:* Fully managed by RevenuePoint. Completely yours to use.

*Subhead:* Pipelines run. Dashboards stay current. Agents execute. You use the portal. We handle everything behind it.

*Body:* A named RevenuePoint administrator manages your Foundry instance — building dashboards, maintaining pipelines, configuring agents, and handling every update. One monthly fee covers all of it. One person who knows your business and answers when you call.

**Pillar Do's**
- Name what's managed specifically (pipelines, dashboards, agents, updates)
- Name the administrator model — it's the humanizing proof point
- Use "Fully managed by RevenuePoint" — not variations of "you use it / we run it"

**Pillar Don'ts**
- Don't lead with cost comparisons ("saves $180K vs. a data engineer") — lead with the model
- Don't frame the client as passive — they use the portal, make decisions, set priorities
- Don't use "white-glove" as the lead descriptor — too vague and too luxury-hotel

### Pillar 3: Action, Not Observation

**Statement:** Foundry doesn't just show you what's happening — it responds to it. Agents watch your business, surface anomalies, execute actions in connected systems, and close the loop between data and work.

**The Underlying Insight**
Every BI tool built in the last 20 years solved the same half of the problem: visualization. They got good at showing you a scrap-rate spike, a churned customer, or a past-due invoice. Then they stopped. The human in the loop had to email someone, file a ticket, or hope someone noticed the dashboard before it was too late. That gap — between observation and response — is where operational problems compound. Foundry is the first managed platform at this price point that closes that loop: the agent that surfaces the problem also executes the response.

**Proof Points**
- Otto executes actions in connected systems — not just surfaces data. Ask Otto to put an order on hold and it happens, with a full audit trail.
- Automated agents run 24/7: Watcher agents fire when inventory drops below threshold; Processor agents convert inbound POs to sales orders; Responder agents triage cases and notify the right rep; Scheduler agents deliver your morning briefing before you arrive.
- Every action is logged, timestamped, and attributed. Human override is always available. Nothing happens without an auditable record.

**Example Copy**

*Headline:* Dashboards describe. Foundry acts.

*Subhead:* Agents watch your business around the clock — and when something matters, they don't just alert you. They respond.

*Body:* When inventory drops below threshold, your watcher agent fires — not an email reminder you might miss. When a customer goes overdue, the right rep is notified and the action is logged. Ask Otto to update a record in your ERP; it happens in seconds. All of it traced. All of it auditable. All of it reversible.

**Pillar Do's**
- Name specific agent types (Watcher, Processor, Responder, Scheduler) with real operational examples
- Emphasize auditability — it's the trust signal that overcomes AI anxiety in regulated industries
- Use short declaratives ("Dashboards describe. Foundry acts.")
- Include human override language to address control concerns

**Pillar Don'ts**
- Don't lead with "agentic AI" — the word is saturated; lead with what the agent does
- Don't imply full autonomy without human confirmation framing where appropriate
- Don't list agent capabilities without operational examples — show, don't enumerate

### Pillar 4: Built Around Your Business

**Statement:** Foundry doesn't reason over database tables. It reasons over your business — Orders, Customers, Invoices, Prescriptions, Production Orders — as you understand them, mapped across every system you run on.

**The Underlying Insight**
Every BI tool connects to your database and gives you access to tables. But your database doesn't know that "Customer" in your CRM and "Account" in your accounting platform are the same entity. It doesn't know that "Production Order" spans your ERP, your MES, and your WMS. It doesn't know what "overdue" means in your specific AR workflow. Every dashboard builder has to re-solve these joins from scratch. Blueprint, Foundry's semantic layer, maps those relationships once — so every dashboard, every AI report, and every agent query draws from the same consistent understanding of your business. Otto doesn't query raw tables; Otto understands what a Customer is.

**Proof Points**
- Blueprint maps business objects across all connected systems once. Every downstream capability — Otto, Lens dashboards, Prism reports, agent conditions — inherits that understanding.
- Industry-specific object models ship with Foundry: manufacturing clients get Production Orders, Defect Logs, and Shift Reports pre-mapped. Nonprofits get Donors, Campaigns, and Gift records. Property managers get Leases, Units, and Maintenance Work Orders.
- "Foundry knows your business" is not a metaphor. When a Salesforce field is renamed, Blueprint absorbs the change. Downstream intelligence stays accurate without manual dashboard rebuilds.

**Example Copy**

*Headline:* Foundry knows your business.

*Subhead:* Not just your data — your business. Orders, invoices, production runs, prescriptions — mapped across every system, so intelligence means something.

*Body:* When Otto answers a question, it answers in the language of your business. When Lens shows margin by product line, it pulls from your ERP, your CRM, and your warehouse simultaneously — without rebuilding every time something changes upstream. Blueprint maps your business objects once. Everything downstream gets that context automatically.

**Pillar Do's**
- Name specific business objects relevant to the reader's industry (tailor for vertical content)
- Contrast with "raw database tables" — concrete and relatable to buyers who've used raw BI tools
- Connect Blueprint explicitly to Otto and dashboards — it's the invisible enabler

**Pillar Don'ts**
- Don't lead with "semantic layer" — too technical; lead with "Foundry knows your business"
- Don't explain the architecture unless the buyer is technical and in a validation context
- Don't use "single source of truth" — use "one consistent model" if needed

### Pillar 5: Speed to Real Value, Not Speed to Setup

**Statement:** Foundry goes live in 6 weeks. The first dashboard is live in 2. Those are commitments, not estimates — because we've done this before, and we price by the outcome, not the hour.

**The Underlying Insight**
Every platform says "set up in minutes." None of them mean it. Mid-market buyers have been burned by "quick starts" that turned into 6-month implementation projects with unexpected professional services bills. The answer is not to promise faster setup — it is to promise specific, believable milestones and deliver them. "Live in 6 weeks. First dashboard in 2" is credible because it is specific. It invites scrutiny. And it reflects a delivery model that is designed for speed: discovery happens before the contract, so implementation begins with clarity, not discovery debt. Flat pricing removes the incentive to extend timelines.

**Proof Points**
- RevenuePoint's discovery process runs before the contract. We learn your systems, your data sources, and your goals before quoting. Implementation begins without the back-half of a typical "discovery phase."
- Client results in weeks, not quarters: $214K in recovered cash flow identified in Q1; customer concentration risk surfaced within 30 days of go-live.
- Flat monthly pricing means our incentive is aligned with yours. We don't bill hourly. We don't extend timelines. Every day you're not live is a day we're absorbing, not billing.

**Example Copy**

*Headline:* Live in 6 weeks. First dashboard in 2.

*Subhead:* We commit to milestones because we've done this before. Discovery. Configuration. Delivery. Management — on a fixed monthly fee that keeps our incentives aligned with your results.

*Body:* Most implementations are sold by the hour. Ours isn't. We price by the month because our success depends on yours. Discovery happens before you sign. Implementation follows a proven sequence. And when we say 6 weeks, we mean it — not as an aspiration, but as the number we've built the delivery model around.

**Pillar Do's**
- Name specific milestone timelines — 6 weeks to live; 2 weeks to first dashboard
- Connect fixed pricing to incentive alignment — we don't bill hourly, so we have no motive to extend
- Include a proof point (ROI metric) wherever this pillar is invoked

**Pillar Don'ts**
- Don't say "fast" or "quickly" — always name the specific commitment
- Don't promise "in minutes" or "instantly" — destroys credibility with buyers who've been burned
- Don't lead with timeline as the primary message — lead with value, support with speed

## 6. Audience Personas & Messaging Matrix

### Persona 1: COO / VP of Operations

**Who They Are**
The operational leader at a manufacturing plant, distribution center, pharmacy group, or multi-site service company. They own production, fulfillment, service delivery, and the metrics that capture how well operations are running. They are the most likely to feel the direct pain of fragmented data — they live in the spreadsheet reconciliation nightmare every week. They often have a technical lean (they understand systems) but are not data engineers. They've seen bad ERP implementations, bad BI rollouts, and bad "AI" demos. They are skeptical and specific.

**What They Care About**
- Operational KPIs that are accurate, current, and actionable: OEE, scrap, throughput, on-time delivery, fill rate
- Visibility across shifts, locations, and departments without manual aggregation
- Automation of repetitive operational responses (reorder, alert, escalate)
- Not being caught off guard by a problem that was visible in the data three weeks ago

**Their #1 Fear**
Making a decision — or presenting to the CEO or board — on data that turns out to be wrong. The operational failure that could have been caught if the data had been right, current, and visible. The scrap-rate spike that nobody noticed for two weeks because the dashboard was stale.

**The Message That Cuts Through**
"Foundry gives you the operational view your team actually needs — dashboards that are always current, agents that watch for conditions you define, and the ability to act without waiting for a report. We build it, we manage it, and we keep it accurate as your business changes."

**Language Register**
- Comfortable with operational terminology: OEE, COGS, throughput, run rate, variance, defect rate
- Prefers concrete metrics over conceptual claims
- Respects specificity — "live in 6 weeks" lands better than "fast"
- Responds to accountability language: "named administrator," "you call them directly"
- Does not need AI explained to them — skip the AI 101 framing

**Opening Lines That Work**
- "Your morning OEE number is only as good as the data it's built on."
- "Operations runs on visibility. Most mid-market ops teams are flying half-blind."
- "When was the last time your dashboard showed you a problem before it showed up in the P&L?"

### Persona 2: CFO / VP of Finance

**Who They Are**
The financial steward at a company between $10M and $250M in revenue. They own the numbers — AR, AP, cash flow, margin by product line, revenue recognition, and the board reporting package. They are the buyer most likely to ask "what does this cost and what do we get?" They have an appetite for AI if it reduces manual work and improves reporting accuracy, but they are viscerally allergic to overpromising. ## 8. Competitive Differentiation

### The Competitive Landscape

Foundry's real competition is not a single product — it's a category decision. Most mid-market companies considering Foundry are choosing between:

1. A self-serve BI platform (Domo, ThoughtSpot, Power BI, Tableau)
2. Hiring a data engineer and building their own stack
3. Using what's already inside their ERP
4. Doing nothing and continuing with spreadsheets and manual reporting
5. Contracting a BI consultant or systems integrator

The orchestration differentiator is Foundry's most defensible position against all of these: no competitor in this segment manages the full loop from data ingestion through intelligence through automated action as a single managed service. This is the claim to own.

---

### Competitor-by-Competitor Position

**Domo**

*What they offer:* Cloud BI with pre-built connectors, dashboards, and some AI features. Self-serve in the sense that customers configure it themselves.

*The gap:* Domo is a visualization and reporting tool. It doesn't manage pipelines, doesn't run agents that take action in connected systems, doesn't have a semantic layer that understands business objects, and requires a dedicated administrator to maintain. It is priced for enterprise ($100K+/year) and designed for companies with BI teams. Mid-market companies buying Domo typically end up with expensive dashboards that nobody maintains and a stale data problem they paid to recreate.

*Battlecard message:* "Domo is a great tool if you have someone to run it. Foundry includes the team."

*What NOT to say:* Don't dismiss Domo on price alone — their enterprise pricing is $100K+, but they have lower-tier options. Focus on the absence of managed service and the absence of agents/actions as the differentiator.

---

**ThoughtSpot**

*What they offer:* AI-powered analytics with natural language search, liveboards, and embedded analytics. Best-in-class NLQ interface.

*The gap:* ThoughtSpot is a query and visualization tool. It averages $140,000/year and requires a data engineering team to connect and maintain it. There are no managed pipelines, no automated agents, no action execution, and no managed service model. It is designed for enterprise BI teams at companies with dedicated data functions.

*Battlecard message:* "ThoughtSpot answers questions beautifully. Foundry answers them and then acts on what it finds — for less than half the cost, with no data engineering team required."

*What NOT to say:* Don't call ThoughtSpot overpriced — the comparison works better as a capability comparison than a price attack.

---

**Power BI / Tableau / Looker (Microsoft, Salesforce)**

*What they offer:* Best-of-breed visualization tools. Power BI has deep Microsoft ecosystem integration. Tableau has the most powerful visualization layer. Looker has the strongest semantic layer for data teams.

*The gap:* All three require a data engineer or dedicated BI developer to build and maintain. Power BI and Tableau are visualization tools — they don't manage pipelines, run agents, or execute in connected systems. Looker comes closest to having a semantic layer but is priced for enterprise and requires full data team support.

*Battlecard message:* "Power BI/Tableau/Looker are excellent tools if you have someone to build and maintain them. Most mid-market companies don't. Foundry includes everything those tools require and everything they're missing — on a flat monthly fee."

*What NOT to say:* Don't say Power BI is bad — it's not, and buyers who've used it know it. Say it's a tool, not a service. The absence of management is the problem.

---

**Hiring a Data Engineer + Stack**

*The scenario:* "We could hire a data engineer, set up Fivetran + Snowflake + dbt + Looker, and own the infrastructure ourselves."

*The reality:* A junior-to-mid data engineer costs $90,000–$140,000/year in fully loaded compensation in North America. They need 3–6 months to build the initial stack. That stack then requires ongoing maintenance, vendor relationship management, and expertise across 5–7 different tools. One engineer cannot cover all of it — and they'll leave. Turnover in data engineering is among the highest in tech. When they leave, the institutional knowledge of the stack leaves with them.

*Battlecard message:* "Hiring for this problem means hiring a person, not solving a problem. One data engineer can't cover ingestion, transformation, orchestration, visualization, semantic modeling, and agent configuration simultaneously — and when they leave, you start over. Foundry is the team, not the person."

*What NOT to say:* Don't make this purely a cost argument — some buyers have the budget for a hire. Lead with continuity, coverage, and speed. The cost comparison is a supporting point.

---

**ERP Native Reporting (SAP, NetSuite, QuickBooks)**

*The scenario:* "We already have reporting inside SAP / NetSuite / QuickBooks."

*The reality:* ERP native reporting shows you what's in the ERP — not what's in the CRM, not what's in the warehouse management system, not what's in the support tool. It reports on its own data model, which is often too technical for non-ERP users. It doesn't cross systems, doesn't write narrative analysis, doesn't run agents, and doesn't execute actions outside its own database.

*Battlecard message:* "ERP reporting shows you your ERP. Foundry shows you your business — across every system you run on — and responds to what it finds."

*What NOT to say:* Don't dismiss the ERP — we often manage SAP for the same clients. The message is "and" not "instead of."

---

**Doing Nothing (Status Quo)**

*The scenario:* "We're managing with spreadsheets and manual exports. It's working well enough."

*The reality:* "Working well enough" is a scar tissue response from a buyer who has been burned before and decided the pain of staying is less than the risk of changing. The cost of the status quo is invisible until it isn't: a board presentation on wrong data, a past-due invoice that went unnoticed for 60 days, a scrap rate that was visible in the data three weeks before anyone addressed it.

*Battlecard message:* "The cost of doing nothing isn't zero — it's the decisions made on data that's late, wrong, or incomplete. Most of our clients couldn't quantify that cost until Foundry surfaced it. One client identified $214K in recoverable cash flow in their first quarter."

*What NOT to say:* Don't belittle the status quo or make the buyer feel foolish for not acting sooner. Name the cost specifically using a proof point.

---

### What NOT to Say When Competitors Come Up

- Don't compare on price alone. Price without capability context loses every time.
- Don't say competitors are "bad tools." They're often good tools in the wrong context. The context is wrong for mid-market buyers who don't have a data team.
- Don't overstate Foundry's AI. Otto is excellent and genuinely useful. It is not omniscient. Don't promise outcomes that depend on data quality we don't control.
- Don't say "we're the only [X]" without being specific. "The only fully managed platform that runs the full orchestration loop" is defensible. "The only AI platform" is not.

## 10. Copy Best Practices & Style Guide

### Sentence Structure Guidelines

**For hero copy:**
- Lead declarative. One strong statement that frames the world or the problem.
- Follow with a pivot or proof. One sentence of context or specificity.
- Close with the solution. One sentence that names what Foundry does.

Example:
> Your CRM, ERP, and accounting system each tell a different story. Reconciling them takes days — and even then, nobody's sure which number is right. Foundry connects every system into one managed layer. One consistent view. Always current.

---

**For headers:**
- Short and declarative. 3–7 words.
- Active voice. Verb-first when possible.
- No questions as headers. Questions signal uncertainty; declarations signal confidence.
- Avoid adverbs and superlatives in headers.

Good: "Data that acts." / "Fully managed by RevenuePoint." / "Live in 6 weeks."
Avoid: "Revolutionize Your Data Strategy" / "Why Foundry Is the Best Platform" / "Are You Getting the Most From Your Data?"

---

**For CTAs:**
- Name what happens next. "Schedule a 30-Minute Demo" is better than "Get Started."
- One CTA per section. Multiple CTAs dilute all of them.
- Consistent language across the site. Standardize on "Schedule a Demo" for Foundry; "Get in Touch" for Salesforce/SAP inquiries.
- Avoid: "Learn More" (too passive), "Try for Free" (doesn't match a managed service model), "Contact Us" (too ambiguous).

---

### Handling Technical Claims Without Losing Non-Technical Buyers

**Rule:** Name the technology when it builds confidence; skip it when it builds distance.

- In hero and problem sections: no technology names. "We connect every system you run on" beats "We use Airbyte and Airflow."
- In "how it works" and architecture sections: name the stack. "Foundry runs on Airbyte for connectors, Airflow for orchestration, dbt for transformation, and Postgres for your warehouse" is credible and specific for the IT buyer who evaluates the technical architecture.
- For Blueprint: never lead with "semantic layer." Lead with what it means: "Foundry knows your business — Orders, Customers, Invoices — not just your database tables." Blueprint is the mechanism; "Foundry knows your business" is the message.
- For agents: never lead with "agentic AI." Lead with what the agent does. "A watcher agent fires when inventory drops below threshold" is concrete. "Our agentic AI layer monitors operational KPIs" is noise.

---

### B2B SaaS Copy Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Opening with features instead of outcomes**
The most common mistake in sub-product copy. Lens is not "curated operational dashboards and interactive metric trees." Lens is "every role sees the metrics that matter." Start with the benefit; follow with the mechanism.

**2. Saying "seamless" or "effortless" integration**
Buyers don't believe it. "Seamless" signals either a broken product or a lie about complexity. Say what we manage and who manages it — that's more credible than "seamless."

**3. Using AI as an adjective without proof**
"AI-powered dashboards" means nothing. "Otto writes a narrative analysis of your warehouse data overnight, with specific numbers and a recommended action" means something. Name what the AI does, not that it exists.

**4. Vague ROI claims without context**
"Save time and money" is meaningless. "$214K recovered in Q1" is a conversation starter. Always anchor ROI claims to a specific outcome, a specific context, and a specific timeframe.

**5. The "but not you" trap**
Saying "ThoughtSpot is designed for enterprise data teams" implies that Foundry is the lesser version. Never position Foundry as the mid-market substitute for an enterprise product. Foundry is the right product for this buyer. It's not Palantir for small companies — it's the platform that was never built for this segment before.

2. The problem they had: specific, operational, personal — name the human who felt it
3. What Foundry surfaced or enabled: specific outcome with a number
4. What changed in how they operate: the "after" state
5. One direct quote if available

**Voice:** First-person narrative. The case study is about the client, not Foundry. Foundry is mentioned as the tool; the outcome is the story.

**What to avoid:** "Using Foundry's AI-powered platform, the client was able to leverage cutting-edge insights to transform their operations." This is everything wrong with case study copy. Name the person, name the problem, name the number.

## 12. Messaging Evolution Roadmap

### Phase 1: Category Creation (Now — 12 months)

**Goal:** Establish "fully managed data orchestration" as a category that Foundry owns. Create awareness that the problem is real, that the alternatives are inadequate, and that Foundry is the specific answer.

**Messaging focus:**
- Lead with the problem (fragmented data, no data team, observation without action)
- Name the category explicitly: "fully managed data orchestration and AI platform"
- Lead with "Foundry is the only managed platform in this segment that runs the full loop from ingestion to action"
- Use ROI proof points from early clients heavily — specific numbers are the category-creation mechanism

**KPIs for this phase:**
- Is "fully managed data orchestration" appearing in prospect conversation and inbound search?
- Are prospects arriving able to articulate why Foundry is different from Domo or ThoughtSpot?
- Are COO and CFO buyers booking demos, not just data/IT leaders?

---

### Phase 2: Proof-Led Growth (12–30 months)

**Goal:** Shift from "here's what we promise" to "here's what clients have achieved." The case study portfolio becomes the primary trust engine.

**Messaging focus:**
- Lead hero sections with client outcomes, not platform capabilities
- Develop vertical-specific landing pages (e.g., /foundry/manufacturing/, /foundry/pharmacy/) with industry-specific proof points
- Build an ROI calculator or interactive estimator for the pricing page
- Introduce a "Foundry in 30 days" narrative: what clients typically see in their first month

**What changes:**
- Replace "Live in 6 weeks" with client-attributed speed proofs: "A manufacturing client was live in 4 weeks."
- Replace generic capability descriptions with outcome-specific case studies within product sections
- Add industry analyst or third-party validation where available

---

### Phase 3: Vertical Depth (30+ months)

**Goal:** Own specific verticals where proof is deepest and the buyer community is connected enough for referral to matter.

**Messaging focus:**
- Industry-specific campaigns and landing pages with named client outcomes
- Vertical-specific sub-positioning (Foundry for Manufacturing, Foundry for Compounding Pharmacy, etc.)
- Partner with vertical trade associations or publications for credibility in-market
- Sub-brand extension: consider whether certain industries warrant dedicated capability branding (e.g., "Foundry for Pharmacy" as a sub-brand with its own site section)

### Signals That Mean It's Time to Revisit the Framework

**Revisit positioning if:**
- A direct competitor launches a managed orchestration model at comparable pricing (the category differentiation narrows)
- Client profiles shift significantly (e.g., Foundry starts landing at 1,000-employee companies — language may need adjustment)
- The "no data team required" premise becomes false (if clients increasingly have data teams, the managed model needs reframing)

**Revisit voice if:**
- Buyer research reveals a shift in the primary persona (e.g., IT Directors start owning the purchase decision instead of COOs/CFOs)
- The "agentic" category matures to the point where claiming "agents that take action" is no longer differentiating — find the next dimension

**Revisit proof points if:**
- A new ROI metric from the client base dramatically outperforms the current benchmarks
- A case study emerges that reframes what's possible (e.g., a client who automated 40% of their AR process) — build the framework around that proof

**Annual review trigger:** Each April, review Sections 2, 4, and 8 against the competitive landscape. Category messaging moves fast in AI. What was differentiating in April 2026 may be table stakes by April 2027.
